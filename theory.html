Microwave Transmission

Microwaves are electromagnetic waves in the frequency range of 1 GHz to 1 THz. In Computer Networks, microwave transmission is widely used for long-distance wireless communication because it supports high data rates and requires no physical cable.

Microwave communication works mainly on the principle of Line-of-Sight (LoS), which means the transmitting and receiving antennas must be able to "see" each other without any major obstruction.

Types of Microwave Communication

1. Terrestrial Microwave

Terrestrial microwave communication uses ground-based towers with parabolic dish antennas. The signal is transmitted in a straight line from one tower to another, usually with the help of repeater stations.

  • Frequency Range: Typically 4–6 GHz, 11 GHz, 18 GHz, 21–23 GHz bands.
  • Transmission Type: Line-of-Sight (LoS).
  • Bandwidth: High bandwidth, suitable for high-speed data, voice, and video.
  • Advantages: No cables required, fast deployment, high data rate.
  • Disadvantages: Affected by rain, fog, and obstacles; requires tall towers.

2. Satellite Microwave

Satellite communication uses an artificial satellite orbiting the Earth as a repeater. The earth station sends a signal to the satellite called the uplink. The satellite amplifies it and sends it back to another earth station called the downlink.

  • Coverage: Very large area (can cover entire countries or continents).
  • Advantage: Long-distance communication without many repeaters.
  • Disadvantage: High propagation delay and high initial cost.
  • Typical Lifetime: 12–15 years.

Infrared (IR) Transmission

Infrared waves are electromagnetic waves with frequencies between 300 GHz and 400 THz. In computer networks, infrared is mainly used for short-range communication inside a room.

Infrared communication is also a Line-of-Sight or reflected communication and behaves similar to light.

Characteristics of Infrared Communication

  • Short Range: Works only over small distances.
  • High Security: Cannot penetrate walls, so signals stay inside a room.
  • High Data Rate: Supports very high bandwidth.
  • Limitation: Affected by sunlight, dust, smoke, and obstacles.

Common example: TV remote control, wireless mouse, short-range sensors.

Important Concepts in Wireless Communication (CN)

1. Line of Sight (LoS)

Line of Sight means there must be a clear, unobstructed path between transmitter and receiver. Microwave and Infrared both mainly require LoS communication.

2. Noise and Interference

Noise is any unwanted signal that disturbs the original signal. Interference occurs when multiple signals overlap. Both reduce signal quality and data rate.

3. Path Loss

Path loss refers to the reduction in signal strength as the signal travels through space due to distance, absorption, and obstacles. In microwave systems, path loss increases with distance and frequency.

4. Bandwidth vs Frequency

Higher frequency bands generally support higher bandwidth and higher data rates, but they also suffer from higher attenuation and require better Line-of-Sight conditions.

5. Propagation Delay

Propagation delay is the time taken by a signal to travel from sender to receiver. Satellite communication has a large propagation delay because the signal travels thousands of kilometers to space and back.
